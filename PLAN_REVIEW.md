# Training Plan Review: GPT Plan vs. the Research

Reviewed July 11, 2026. Sources at the bottom.

**Laurence's decisions, July 11:** The plantar fasciitis label was a misdiagnosis; working theory is a minor midfoot stress fracture with the differential still open. Pain has improved over the recent no-running weeks, so a cautious test run is planned within 1-2 weeks (short walk/run, soft surface, stop at any pain, judge the 24-48h response before more). Aqua jogging is out; zone 2 comes from easy swims and the bike. The seed data reflects all three calls.

## What the GPT plan gets right

The A-F rotation matches concurrent-training research on several points. You lift before you do endurance work on mixed days, which preserves neuromuscular adaptation. You separate bench days from pull days. The runner-durability block (RDLs, Bulgarians, single-leg RDLs, tibialis/calf work) targets the exact tissues a return to running loads first. The rows-before-pull-ups rule is a real, order-dependent effect you measured yourself, and the plan honors it. Heavy pressing at 175-195 lb also serves your running: meta-analyses show heavy resistance training improves running economy more than any other strength method (pooled effect g = -0.32, roughly double plyometrics).

## Where the plan has gaps

**1. No return-to-running plan exists at all.** The plan says "no running until cleared" and stops there. Clinical protocols for metatarsal stress fractures run 6-8 weeks of offloading, then a graduated walk/run progression over 3-4 more weeks, with two clearance gates: no tenderness on direct palpation plus a pain-free single-leg calf raise, and imaging confirmation for anything suspicious. Your injury started around early June. If imaging clears you in August, a structured progression puts you at continuous easy running by mid-September. That timeline matters for gap #2.

**2. The December ultra math is tight and the plan never runs it.** A first 50K wants 12-16 weeks of running build, peaking at 35-50 miles per week, with long runs progressing to 4-5 hours. Counting back from a mid-December race, the build must start by early September at the latest. That leaves zero buffer for a slow-healing fracture or a setback during return-to-run. Nobody in the GPT transcript flagged this. Decisions to make now: get imaging and a clinical opinion this month, and pick a fallback (a February/March ultra, or a December race you hike-run conservatively). The coach should track this decision as a goal with a deadline.

**3. Aqua jogging is missing from the cardio substitutions.** Swimming and cycling maintain general fitness, but deep-water running preserves about 90% of VO2max with running-specific movement patterns, and studies show runners hold 2-mile performance for 6+ weeks with it. You have pool access three rotations a week. Adding one aqua-jogging session (or replacing one swim) protects your running fitness better than either current modality. Cycling research shows it transfers, but water running transfers more directly to stride mechanics.

**4. Bone health goes unaddressed.** You have a suspected stress fracture and a history of plantar fasciitis, and the plan says nothing about why the bone failed. Ask for a vitamin D level at the same visit as imaging (below ~76 nmol/L raises fracture risk). Check calcium intake against the ~1,500 mg/day recommendation for athletes with bone stress injuries. Also worth an honest look at energy availability: you were cutting toward a 10 lb loss while ramping mileage, and low energy availability is a primary driver of bone stress injury in endurance athletes. A deliberate maintenance-calorie block through healing is cheap insurance.

**5. Zone 2 volume is thin for an ultra base.** The E slot (60-90 min bike or long swim) appears once per rotation. Ultra preparation is mostly about time at low intensity. While you can't run, two low-intensity sessions per rotation (one long bike, one long swim or aqua jog) build the aerobic base you'll need in September without adding bone load. The swim-threshold session (C) is good but it is the intensity session, not the base.

**6. Interference management can be sharper.** Research supports separating hard endurance work from heavy lower-body lifting by 3+ hours, and ideally by a day. The current rotation mostly does this by accident. Make it a rule: no swim-threshold or long-bike day immediately after lower-strength day if RDLs went heavy, and keep the easy sessions genuinely easy (the July 7 swim averaged HR 140 with 35% of time in zone 4, which is a tempo day, not a base day, and it was correctly labeled tempo but the plan lacks a true easy swim).

**7. Plyometrics belong in the plan later, not now.** Once the foot clears impact, low-dose plyometrics (pogo hops, skipping, low box jumps) improve running economy at easy paces and re-load the bone progressively. Sequence them into the return-to-run phase, after the walk/run progression and before speed work. Doing this now would be reckless; skipping it forever wastes a proven adaptation.

## What I'd change in the rotation

Current: A Upper Strength, B Lower Strength, C Swim Threshold, D Upper Pull, E Long Zone 2, F Upper Strength.

Proposed while injured: keep A, B, D, F as written. Split C into one true threshold swim and one easy technique swim. Expand E to two zone-2 slots (long bike + easy swim; Laurence passed on aqua jogging). Result over 8 days: 3 lifts, 1 threshold swim, 3 easy aerobic sessions, 1 rest day (midweek, per your preference). That is one more aerobic touch than the current plan without new bone load, and it fits the 50-75 minute session cap.

After clearance: walk/run replaces one zone-2 slot, then grows. Lifting drops to 2x/week at the same loads (research shows maintaining strength takes far less volume than building it), which frees recovery for the running build.

## Bottom line

The strength programming is solid and better than most hybrid templates. The endurance side is underbuilt for a December ultra, the injury plan has no exit ramp, and nothing addresses why the bone broke. Fix those three and the plan is genuinely strong.
